\section{Evaluation \& Results}

\paragraph{Baseline Performance.}
As mentioned in our Methodology section, our corruption framework requires the raw data to perform
the various corruption transformations on it. However, the processed data which does not include
the time component produces much higher accuracy and macro-$F_1$ scores due to the fact that many more
features are generated. We performed baseline experiments on clean train and test data for our various
models. With the classical models, we achieved accuracy ranging from 50-60\% which is expected for
these models. However, the LSTM model performs at close to 91.0\% accuracy using the raw
data, which is comparable to Saha et al. \cite{saha-2025} with their LSTM model where they achieved
93.89\% accuracy (with imputation).

\paragraph{Effect of Dropout.}
Figure \ref{fig:dropout} outlines the effects of dropout corruption. The macro-$F_1$ score decreases
significantly only for the KNN model, while for the other models (logistic, SVM, LSTM) the macro-$F_1$
score decreases steadily. This supports our earlier point that dropout corruption is currently the
only corruption type that is being studied in literature and does not significantly decrease
accuracy or macro-$F_1$ score except up to the point that dropout corruption removes test data.

\paragraph{Effect of Drift.}
Figure \ref{fig:drift} outlines the effects of drift corruption. This corruption type significantly
affects the models. As severity increases, we can observe that the macro-$F_1$ score significantly
decreases for all models. LSTM is the model most negatively affected by the corruption. The reason
why drift corruption has a more negative impact than the dropout corruption is because drift
corruption corrupts every timestep in the signal window with a monotonically increasing offset.
This fundamentally alters the properties of the signal, it no longer preserves the integrity of
underlying signal making it increasingly unrecognizable to models that rely on learned temporal
features.

\paragraph{Effect of Gain.}
Gain corruption is separated into amplification (gain $> 1$) and attenuation (gain $< 1$). Note
that gain $= 1$ is the baseline performance without any corruption.

Figure \ref{fig:attenuation} outlines the effects when gain $< 1$. As the severity decreases (the
corruption becomes more significant), we can observe that the macro-$F_1$ score decreases for some
models. We can observe that the logistic, SVM, and LSTM models are largely unaffected by
attenuation. The only model significantly affected by attenuation is KNN. The reason is that
attenuation scales the test signal values down uniformly, moving them away from the region of the
feature space occupied by the clean training data. KNN relies on distances to training examples, so
this shift causes test points to be matched against incorrect neighbors. Logistic regression, SVM,
and LSTM are less affected because they learn decision boundaries that are more invariant to
uniform scaling of the input.

\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{plots/trend_Gain_(attenuation_lt_1)_Macro-F1.png}
    \caption{Macro-$F_1$ score for the gain corruption (attenuation) on the test dataset on the
    various models.}
    \label{fig:attenuation}
\end{figure}

Figure \ref{fig:amplification} outlines the effects when gain $> 1$.
Amplification scales the test signal values up uniformly, moving test points further away from the
origin in the feature space. Logistic regression and SVM are largely unaffected because their
decision boundaries are defined by the direction of feature differences rather than their absolute
magnitude, making them inherently more invariant to uniform scaling. Surprisingly, KNN macro-$F_1$
scores increase as severity increases, which is the inverse of the attenuation effect. A plausible
explanation is that the clean training and test data are not perfectly aligned in scale.
Amplification may move test samples closer to their true class cluster in the training set. This
is an unexpected finding that should be investigated in future work.

\end{figure}

\paragraph{Effect of Bias.}
Figure \ref{fig:bias} outlines the effects of bias corruption. This corruption type also
significantly affects the models. As severity increases, we can observe that the macro-$F_1$ score
decreases significantly for all models. Similarly with drift corruption, LSTM is the model most
affected by the bias corruption. The reason why bias corruption has a significant impact is because
bias corruption shifts the entire signal by a constant offset, moving it outside the distribution
the models were trained on. A constant offset displaces every timestep, which causes the model's
learned feature representations to no longer correspond to the corrupted input. Again, this
highlights the importance of training models robust to additional corruption types beyond dropout.

\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{plots/trend_Bias_Macro-F1.png}
    \caption{Macro-$F_1$ score for the bias corruption on the test dataset on the various models.}
    \label{fig:bias}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Effect of Stochastic Noise.}
Stochastic noise had minimal impact on performance. This is because stochastic noise only corrupts
a subset of timesteps within the signal window and only displaces these timesteps by a small random
amount scaled to the signals standard deviation. Since the noise is applied independently for each
timestep corrupted, it tends to average out over the window, leaving the overall signal structure
largely intact. Models trained on clean data are typically already robust to this type of
perturbation as the corrupted signal remains similar to what was seen during training.
